Working Paper 003 — From Indicators to Worlds: Measuring Civic Life Under Fragmented Authority

Measurement appears neutral. In practice, it carries assumptions about the world it seeks to describe.

Much contemporary research on civic engagement, trust, and public life relies on survey indicators that travel across countries and cities. These indicators are often technically clear and statistically robust. Yet their conceptual portability is less frequently examined.

This working paper asks a simple but uncomfortable question: when we measure civic life in institutional environments marked by fragmentation, uneven state presence, and plural authority, what exactly are our indicators capturing?

1. Indicators are not the world

Indicators are abstractions. They select particular behaviours, expressions, or attitudes and treat them as proxies for broader concepts. Voting becomes a proxy for political participation. Trust in government becomes a proxy for institutional legitimacy. Attendance at meetings becomes a proxy for civic engagement.

These abstractions are often reasonable within relatively coherent institutional settings. But when institutional form varies substantially, the relationship between indicator and concept can shift.

An absence of formal participation may reflect apathy. It may also reflect inaccessibility. Low expressed trust may indicate delegitimation. It may also indicate differentiated evaluation across institutional layers.

The indicator remains the same. The world it maps has changed.

2. Variation as structural signal

Comparative research frequently interprets variation across cities or groups as evidence of attitudinal difference. But once basic demographic composition is accounted for, persistent variation may instead reflect structural divergence in governance ecology.

Differences in exposure to reliable services, administrative accessibility, police behaviour, bureaucratic responsiveness, or crisis recourse shape how people interpret institutions. Residents act not only as bearers of opinion, but as interpreters of patterned experience.

In this sense, variation in survey responses can be read as signal. Not signal of civic virtue or deficiency, but signal of institutional configuration.

3. The risk of misrecognition

When measurement frameworks assume a stable and unified public order, they risk misrecognising contexts where authority is plural and uneven. They may collapse differentiated trust into generalised distrust. They may interpret selective reliance as inconsistency. They may read institutional adaptation as civic withdrawal.

The problem is not statistical. It is conceptual.

Without careful reflection on how institutional design shapes observable behaviour, empirical findings can drift into moral interpretation. Citizens appear disengaged. Communities appear distrustful. Participation appears low. The structural architecture recedes from view.

4. Toward a structure-first measurement posture

A structure-first approach to measurement begins with a different sequence of questions.

First, what forms of authority are present in the setting being studied?

Second, how accessible are these authorities to different groups?

Third, what is the observable reliability of institutional response?

Fourth, how do these features shape the practical incentives attached to formal participation?

Only after these layers are considered should variation in expressed trust or participation be interpreted.

This does not require abandoning standardised instruments. It requires contextualising them. Indicators should be read alongside institutional exposure, service reliability, administrative friction, and crisis response patterns.

5. From comparison to interpretation

Cross-city comparison remains essential. But comparison without conceptual discipline can produce misleading hierarchies. One city appears more engaged, another more distrustful, another more participatory. The ranking obscures the institutional conditions under which these patterns emerge.

A more careful comparative posture treats differences not as immediate judgement, but as prompts for structural inquiry. What differs in governance ecology? Where are access points narrower? Where is recourse more predictable? Where is authority more plural?

6. Conclusion

Indicators are powerful tools. They make variation visible. But they do not speak on their own.

When institutional environments are fragmented or uneven, measurement must be paired with conceptual humility. Without it, we risk mapping categories onto worlds they were not designed to capture.

Civic life does not disappear because it fails to appear in familiar indicators. Sometimes the task is not to measure harder, but to interpret more carefully.
