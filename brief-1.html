Executive Brief 001 — Coordination Risk Before Financial Symptoms

Audience: Board / Non-Executive Directors

Executive Summary

Most material organisational risks emerge before financial symptoms appear. The early indicators are structural: rising internal friction, increased escalation, duplicated effort, and delayed decisions.

These are coordination risks. If left unattended, they compound into performance drag, talent attrition, and credibility erosion.

This brief outlines how to identify coordination risk early and where board oversight is most effective.

1. What the Board Should Be Watching

Financial metrics lag. Coordination metrics lead.

Early structural signals include:

• Increase in cross-functional rework
• Escalation of operational issues to executive level
• Delays attributed to “alignment” rather than capacity
• Repeated revision of decisions already taken
• Rising informal workarounds

These signals suggest the organisation is expending energy maintaining internal alignment rather than advancing strategy.

2. The Economic Impact

Coordination failure does not initially reduce revenue. It reduces efficiency and decision velocity.

Over time, this manifests as:

• Slower execution of strategic initiatives
• Increased cost per output unit
• Risk aversion at middle management levels
• Higher dependency on senior intervention

By the time margin erosion appears, structural misalignment is usually entrenched.

3. A Simple Oversight Metric

Boards do not need operational dashboards to assess coordination health.

Request directional tracking on:

Alignment Drag Ratio = (Rework + Escalation Time + Duplicate Initiatives) ÷ Strategic Delivery Hours

The absolute value is less important than the trend. Rising alignment drag indicates latent execution risk.

4. Key Questions for Management

Boards should ask:

• Where are decision rights unclear?
• What recurring issues consume executive bandwidth?
• Are incentives aligned to enterprise outcomes or unit performance?
• Where does informal coordination replace formal structure?

The aim is not blame. The aim is visibility.

5. Strategic Options

If coordination risk is rising, leadership typically faces three choices:

Stabilise: Add process, reporting, oversight.
Redesign: Clarify decision rights, realign incentives, simplify interfaces.
Absorb: Allow informal systems to continue compensating.

The third option often appears efficient in the short term. It increases fragility over time.

6. Board-Level Implication

Coordination risk is a governance issue.

It affects capital allocation, leadership stability, risk management, and long-term credibility.

Boards that monitor structural alignment early reduce the likelihood of reactive intervention later.

Financial underperformance is often the last signal.

Coordination breakdown is usually the first.
