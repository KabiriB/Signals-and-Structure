The Cost of Coordination Failure

Most organisational losses don’t show up as dramatic failures.

They show up as duplication. As delay. As rework. As meetings about meetings.

The organisation is busy. But it is not aligned.

That gap — between activity and alignment — is the cost of coordination failure.

1. When everyone is working, but not together

Coordination failure is rarely loud.

Marketing launches before operations is ready. Strategy shifts without finance recalibrating. Sales promises timelines engineering never approved.

No one is malicious. No one is incompetent.

The system simply lacks shared visibility and shared timing.

Over time, this misalignment compounds.

2. The hidden economics of misalignment

Coordination failure has a real cost, even when revenue looks stable.

Consider just three forms of leakage:

Redundant work. Two teams solving the same problem in parallel.

Rework. Deliverables revised because upstream assumptions changed.

Escalation drag. Senior leaders pulled into issues that should resolve at lower levels.

None of these appear clearly on a balance sheet.

But together, they form a quiet tax on performance.

3. A simple coordination metric

One practical way to diagnose coordination health is to track what I call Alignment Drag.

Alignment Drag = (Rework Hours + Escalation Time + Duplicate Effort) ÷ Core Delivery Hours

You don’t need perfect measurement. Even directional tracking reveals patterns.

If alignment drag rises while output remains constant, the organisation is burning internal energy to maintain external stability.

That energy eventually runs out.

4. Why coordination fails

Coordination failure usually emerges from structural conditions:

Unclear decision rights.
Incentives that reward local optimisation over system performance.
Information asymmetry between units.
Leadership signalling that changes faster than execution systems can adapt.

Over time, teams begin to protect themselves.

Local wins matter more than shared outcomes. Reporting replaces collaboration.

The organisation becomes a federation of silos.

5. The behavioural echo

Remember Essay 3: behaviour adapts to the game.

In misaligned systems, self-protection becomes rational. Teams hedge. Managers document. Leaders intervene.

Trust narrows.

What began as a coordination gap becomes a cultural story.

“They don’t collaborate.”

Often, the issue is not collaboration. It’s architecture.

6. The leadership move

When coordination failure becomes visible, leaders have two broad options.

Add process.
More meetings. More reporting. More checkpoints.

Or:

Redesign interfaces.
Clarify decision rights. Align incentives. Reduce ambiguity in handoffs.

The first increases administrative load. The second reduces structural friction.

Many organisations default to the first.

It feels safer.

7. The compounding effect

Coordination failure is rarely catastrophic. It is cumulative.

Small misalignments compound quietly until the organisation feels slow, heavy, or reactive.

Leaders then describe the problem as “complexity.”

Often, it is not complexity.

It is uncoordinated complexity.

And that is diagnosable.

The question is whether someone is measuring the drag before it becomes the narrative.
