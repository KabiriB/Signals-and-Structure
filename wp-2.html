Working Paper 002 — Trust, Reliance, and Fragmented Authority in African Cities

In many African urban contexts, public authority is not experienced as a single, coherent structure. It is encountered as a field of overlapping actors: municipal officials, national bureaucracies, police, councillors, religious leaders, local brokers, informal organisers, neighbourhood associations, kinship networks.

People do not move through one public system. They navigate several.

This working paper develops a simple proposition: trust and reliance do not attach uniformly across these actors. They are differentiated. And that differentiation is often read incorrectly.

1. The assumption of coherence

Much institutional analysis begins with an implicit assumption: that the state appears to citizens as a relatively unified object. Under this assumption, measures of trust in “government” or “public institutions” are treated as signals of generalised legitimacy.

But in practice, the state is rarely encountered in aggregate form. It is encountered through offices, officials, frontline actors, and specific interactions. A resident may distrust national political leadership while relying heavily on a local councillor. They may avoid formal administrative channels while turning to police in moments of crisis. They may distrust the municipality but trust religious leadership as a more reliable mediator.

Aggregated trust measures can flatten these distinctions.

2. Trust versus reliance

A useful distinction in fragmented institutional environments is between trust and reliance.

Trust refers to an expectation of fairness, competence, or integrity. Reliance refers to practical use — turning to an actor when a problem must be solved.

These do not always align.

Residents may rely on actors they do not fully trust because alternatives are limited. Conversely, they may express normative trust in institutions they do not practically engage because those institutions remain distant or abstract.

When trust and reliance diverge, it signals something about institutional configuration.

3. Fragmented authority as lived reality

In settings shaped by migration, informality, and uneven state presence, authority is often plural. Formal governance structures coexist with informal intermediaries. Administrative pathways overlap with relational negotiation. Crisis response may be centralised, while everyday problem-solving is local.

This does not necessarily indicate institutional collapse. It indicates differentiation.

The analytical question is not whether fragmentation exists — it often does — but how people interpret and navigate it.

4. Reading variation as signal

Variation across neighbourhoods, groups, or cities in patterns of trust and reliance should not automatically be reduced to cultural disposition or demographic difference. Once basic composition is accounted for, persistent differences may reflect structural variation in access, exposure, service reliability, or administrative responsiveness.

In other words, people may be acting as sensors of governance ecology.

When a community consistently bypasses one institutional actor while turning to another, this is not simply preference. It may reflect accumulated experience about where recourse is available, where promises are kept, and where engagement produces cost without return.

5. The danger of moral interpretation

Fragmented reliance patterns are sometimes described in moral language: clientelism, dependency, apathy, distrustful culture. These labels may capture real dynamics, but they can also obscure structural conditions.

If formal institutions are slow, inaccessible, or unevenly present, turning to relational or localised actors becomes rational. Behaviour that appears normatively problematic may be institutionally adaptive.

The risk of moral interpretation is that it relocates explanation from system design to citizen character.

6. Implications for governance analysis

A more careful approach requires separating three layers:

First, the distribution of authority — who can act, at what scale, with what capacity.

Second, the distribution of access — who can realistically reach which actors, at what cost.

Third, the distribution of reliability — which actors deliver predictably and visibly.

Patterns of trust and reliance emerge from the interaction of these layers. They are not reducible to attitudes alone.

7. Toward a differentiated account of public life

The concept of archipelagic publics is helpful here. It suggests that urban civic life may be organised as a constellation of partially connected but unevenly aligned authority nodes. Residents move between these nodes, not as passive subjects, but as strategic interpreters of risk, opportunity, and institutional credibility.

In such environments, trust is rarely uniform. It is conditional and layered. Reliance is pragmatic and situational. Public authority is plural.

Recognising this does not weaken institutional analysis. It strengthens it. It allows us to see where credibility accumulates, where it erodes, and how people adapt in response.

8. Conclusion

Trust and reliance are not simple reflections of civic virtue or decay. They are patterned responses to the architecture of authority.

If we want to understand public life in African cities, we must pay closer attention to how authority is distributed, how access is structured, and how reliability is experienced in practice.

Fragmented authority is not an analytical inconvenience. It is part of the civic terrain. The task is not to smooth it out conceptually, but to read it carefully.
