Most institutional breakdowns do not begin with failure. They begin with friction. A deadline takes slightly longer than it used to. A workaround becomes normal. A decision stalls, but everyone treats it as temporary. Nothing dramatic. Nothing headline-worthy. Just a subtle increase in the effort required to produce the same result.

That is usually where the story begins.

1. When “working harder” becomes the strategy

In organisations, especially high-performing ones, early warning signs are often mistaken for temporary strain. Leaders respond with urgency. Teams compensate. Meetings multiply, reporting tightens, and people push harder. Output still looks stable. Revenue holds. Projects move. On the surface, everything appears fine.

But beneath that stability, the cost of keeping things stable is rising. That rising cost is rarely tracked directly. It is absorbed — quietly and unevenly — by people across the system. And that invisible accumulation is the signal.

2. The diagnostic mistake

When pressure increases, most leadership teams default to familiar explanations. They look at capacity or effort. “We need more people.” “We need tighter control.” “We need stronger accountability.” Sometimes those explanations are correct.

But often the deeper issue is structural misalignment — incentives that no longer align with outcomes, coordination pathways that no longer hold, decision rights that are unclear, or information that does not flow where it should. In these moments, the system begins to rely on human effort to compensate for design strain.

The danger is not that people work harder. The danger is that this compensation becomes permanent.

3. Reading friction before failure

There is a quiet shift that changes how organisations are understood: stop measuring failure and start measuring friction. Failure appears late, when options are already constrained. Friction appears early, when there is still room to act.

Friction does not announce itself as crisis. It shows up in patterns. Decisions begin to require more approvals than before. Exceptions increase month after month. Informal channels begin to carry work that formal systems cannot. Senior leaders find themselves pulled into operational detail. The same issues escalate repeatedly without resolution.

Individually, none of these signals are dramatic. Together, they form a pattern that reveals how the system is actually functioning.

4. A simple technical lens: Compensation Load

One practical way to make this visible is through a simple diagnostic I call Compensation Load. It is not a complex model. It is a way of making hidden effort legible.

Compensation Load = (Workarounds + Escalations + Manual Overrides) ÷ Core Transactions

The goal is not precision. It is visibility over time. If outputs remain stable while compensation load continues to rise, the system is borrowing its stability from human effort. And borrowed stability is always fragile.

Eventually, fatigue sets in. Talent leaves. Errors begin to compound. What appears, in hindsight, as a sudden crisis is simply the moment the system can no longer sustain the cost of its own workarounds.

It was never sudden.

5. The leadership decision point

When friction becomes visible, leadership teams face a decision, whether they recognise it or not. They can stabilise, redesign, or absorb.

Stabilising adds short-term support, reduces immediate pressure, and buys time. Redesigning addresses the underlying structure — incentives, decision rights, information flow, and coordination architecture. Absorbing allows the system to continue as it is, relying on people to carry the strain.

Many organisations stabilise. Quite a few default to absorption. Very few choose redesign early enough.

Redesign requires a difficult acknowledgment: that the problem is not primarily about individuals, but about the structure within which those individuals are operating.

6. Why this matters in real time

In executive environments, decisions are rarely made under conditions of perfect clarity. They are made under pressure, with incomplete information and limited time. The value of diagnostics is not prediction. It is timing.

Reading signals early creates space to act before the organisation is cornered. It allows leadership to respond with intention rather than urgency. It replaces reaction with choice.

7. A quiet practice

When something begins to feel harder than it used to be, the instinct is often to look for error or blame. A more useful starting point is different.

Ask what is repeating. Ask who is compensating. Ask what conditions make the current outcome rational within the system as it exists.

This sequence moves attention from isolated events to patterns, and from patterns to structure. And structure is where leverage lives.

Institutions rarely collapse without warning. They drift there.

The question is whether someone is reading the drift.
