Reading the Signals Before the Crisis

Most institutional breakdowns don’t begin with failure. They begin with friction.

A deadline takes slightly longer. A workaround becomes normal. A decision stalls — but everyone pretends it’s temporary.

Nothing dramatic. Nothing headline-worthy. Just a small increase in effort required to produce the same result.

That’s usually where the story begins.

1. When “working harder” becomes the strategy

In organisations, especially high-performing ones, early warning signs are often mistaken for temporary strain. Leaders respond with urgency. Teams compensate. Meetings multiply. Reporting tightens. People push.

Output still looks stable. Revenue holds. Projects move.

On the surface, everything is fine.

But beneath that stability, the cost of keeping things stable is rising.

That rising cost — invisible, distributed, quietly absorbed — is the signal.

2. The diagnostic mistake

When pressure increases, most leadership teams default to one of two explanations: capacity or effort.

“We need more people.” “We need tighter control.” “We need stronger accountability.”

Sometimes that’s true.

But often the deeper issue is structural misalignment — incentives, coordination pathways, decision rights, information flow. In other words, the system is asking people to compensate for design strain.

The danger is not that people work harder. The danger is that the compensation becomes permanent.

3. Reading friction before failure

Here’s the quiet shift that changes everything:

Stop measuring failure. Start measuring friction.

Failure shows up late. Friction shows up early.

Friction looks like:

Decisions requiring more approvals than before.
Exceptions increasing month over month.
Informal channels doing work formal systems cannot.
Senior leaders intervening in operational details.
The same problems escalating repeatedly.

None of these are crises. But together, they form a pattern.

4. A simple technical lens: Compensation Load

One practical diagnostic I use in organisations is something I call Compensation Load.

It’s not complicated.

Compensation Load = (Workarounds + Escalations + Manual Overrides) ÷ Core Transactions

You don’t need perfect data. You need trend visibility.

If outputs remain stable while compensation load rises, the system is borrowing stability from human effort.

And borrowed stability is fragile.

Eventually, fatigue sets in. Talent leaves. Errors compound. The “sudden crisis” arrives.

It was never sudden.

5. The leadership decision point

When friction becomes visible, leadership teams usually face three choices.

Stabilise.
Add short-term support. Reduce pressure. Buy time.

Redesign.
Adjust incentives, decision rights, information flow, coordination architecture.

Absorb.
Let people continue compensating and hope resilience holds.

Many organisations choose the first. Quite a few default to the third. Very few choose the second early enough.

Redesign requires acknowledging that the issue is structural, not personal.

6. Why this matters in real time

In executive rooms, decisions are rarely made under perfect clarity. They are made under pressure, incomplete information, and time constraint.

The value of diagnostics is not prediction. It is timing.

Reading signals early gives you room to act before the organisation is cornered.

It replaces panic with choice.

7. A quiet practice

If something in your organisation feels “harder than it used to be,” don’t start with blame.

Ask:

What is repeating?
Who is compensating?
What conditions make this outcome rational?

That sequence moves you from event to pattern to structure.

And structure is where leverage lives.

Institutions rarely collapse without warning. They drift there.

The question is whether someone is reading the drift.
