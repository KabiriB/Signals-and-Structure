The Game Beneath the Behaviour

When behaviour frustrates us, we usually start with character.

“They’re risk-averse.” “They lack ownership.” “There’s no accountability.”

Sometimes that’s true.

But more often, behaviour is simply the visible surface of a deeper game.

And every organisation — whether it admits it or not — is running one.

1. Behaviour is rarely random

In stable systems, behaviour looks predictable. In strained systems, it looks irrational. But even irrational-looking behaviour usually makes sense inside the incentives people face.

If escalation is rewarded more than quiet resolution, people escalate.

If avoiding visible mistakes matters more than delivering outcomes, delay becomes rational.

If informal relationships move work faster than formal channels, informal networks become the real operating system.

From the outside, this can look like dysfunction. From the inside, it is adaptation.

2. The quiet logic of incentives

Every system creates a payoff structure — not always financial, often social.

Promotions, recognition, safety, reputation, access to leadership, avoidance of blame. These are currencies.

People respond to the currency that matters.

The mistake leaders make is assuming that stated values are the same as operative incentives.

They rarely are.

3. A simple diagnostic: What is rewarded in practice?

When behaviour feels misaligned with strategy, ask three questions:

What behaviour gets promoted?
What behaviour gets protected?
What behaviour quietly gets punished?

The answers usually reveal the real game.

For example, if senior leaders intervene in operational detail but claim to empower teams, the system is rewarding upward escalation.

If cost control is praised publicly but revenue growth determines bonuses, growth will win.

This isn’t hypocrisy. It’s structural signalling.

4. The technical layer: Small game theory, real consequences

You don’t need advanced mathematics to understand strategic behaviour. A simple two-choice payoff logic explains a lot.

Imagine two managers deciding whether to share information or hold it back.

If both share, performance improves — but risk exposure increases.

If one withholds while the other shares, the withholder gains leverage.

If both withhold, coordination suffers but personal risk stays low.

In high-blame cultures, withholding becomes rational.

No memo can fix that. Only redesigning incentives can.

5. Why culture talk often fails

Organisations often respond to behavioural frustration with culture programs.

Workshops. Values posters. Off-sites.

These are not useless. But they operate at the narrative layer.

Behaviour is anchored at the incentive layer.

If those two layers conflict, incentives win. They always do.

6. Behaviour as a diagnostic signal

Instead of asking, “Why are people acting like this?”

Ask, “What must be true about the system for this behaviour to make sense?”

That question changes the posture completely.

It moves you from frustration to diagnosis.

It shifts the focus from personalities to structure.

7. The leadership move

When behaviour becomes problematic, leaders have three options:

Correct the person.
Signal new expectations.
Redesign the game.

The first two are visible and fast. The third is slower and harder.

But only the third produces durable change.

People do not behave in isolation. They behave inside systems.

And if you want different behaviour, you must change the game beneath it.
