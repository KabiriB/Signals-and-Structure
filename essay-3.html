The Game Beneath the Behaviour

When behaviour frustrates us, we tend to begin with character. We say people are risk-averse, that they lack ownership, that accountability is missing. Sometimes those explanations are correct. But more often, behaviour is not a reflection of character. It is the visible surface of a deeper game.

And every organisation, whether it recognises it or not, is running one.

1. Behaviour is rarely random

In stable systems, behaviour appears predictable. In strained systems, it can look irrational. But even behaviour that appears irrational usually makes sense when viewed in relation to the incentives people face.

If escalation is rewarded more than quiet resolution, people will escalate. If avoiding visible mistakes matters more than delivering outcomes, delay becomes rational. If informal relationships move work more effectively than formal processes, those relationships become the real operating system.

From the outside, this can look like dysfunction. From the inside, it is adaptation.

2. The quiet logic of incentives

Every system produces a payoff structure. It is not always financial. More often, it is social. Promotion, recognition, safety, reputation, proximity to leadership, and the avoidance of blame all function as currencies within organisations.

People respond to the currency that matters most in practice, not the one that is described in policy or values statements.

This is where many leadership teams misread their own organisations. They assume that stated values are aligned with operative incentives. In reality, the two often diverge. And when they do, behaviour follows incentives.

3. A simple diagnostic: What is rewarded in practice?

When behaviour appears misaligned with strategy, the most useful move is not to question intent, but to examine signals. What behaviour gets promoted? What behaviour gets protected? What behaviour quietly gets punished?

The answers to these questions usually reveal the real structure of the system. If senior leaders intervene frequently in operational detail while speaking about empowerment, the system is rewarding upward escalation. If cost discipline is emphasised publicly but revenue growth drives bonuses, growth will dominate decision-making.

This is not hypocrisy. It is structural signalling.

4. The technical layer: Small game theory, real consequences

Strategic behaviour does not require complex mathematics to understand. Even simple payoff structures can generate predictable patterns. Imagine two managers deciding whether to share information or withhold it.

If both share, coordination improves but individual risk increases. If one withholds while the other shares, the one who withholds gains leverage. If both withhold, coordination suffers but personal exposure remains limited.

In environments where blame is high and trust is low, withholding becomes the rational strategy. No directive or memo can change that. Only a shift in the underlying incentives can.

5. Why culture talk often fails

When behaviour becomes problematic, organisations often respond with culture interventions. Workshops are held, values are restated, and leadership messages are amplified. These efforts are not meaningless, but they operate at the level of narrative.

Behaviour, however, is anchored at the level of incentives. When narrative and incentives are misaligned, incentives win. They do so consistently and predictably.

6. Behaviour as a diagnostic signal

A more useful question than “Why are people acting this way?” is “What must be true about the system for this behaviour to make sense?”

That shift changes the entire posture of analysis. It moves attention away from individuals and toward structure. It replaces frustration with diagnosis.

Once behaviour is understood as a signal rather than a problem in itself, it becomes possible to read the system more clearly.

7. The leadership move

When behaviour becomes misaligned or problematic, leaders have three broad options. They can correct individuals, they can signal new expectations, or they can redesign the system within which behaviour occurs.

The first two options are visible and immediate. The third is slower, less visible, and more difficult to execute. But it is the only approach that produces durable change.

People do not behave in isolation. They behave within structured environments. And if different behaviour is required, the underlying game must change.
