Working Paper 001 — This Is Not Disengagement: Rethinking Civic Life in African Cities

Much of the contemporary literature on civic engagement still carries a narrow image of what public participation should look like. It tends to recognise voting, formal association membership, public meetings, invited participation, petitioning, protest, and direct contact with recognised state actors as the most visible and legitimate forms of civic life.

These measures are not trivial. They tell us something important. But they do not tell us everything. In many African urban contexts, they often tell us less than we think.

This working paper begins from a simple concern: people are too often read as civically absent when, in fact, they are navigating public life through forms that dominant frameworks do not readily recognise. What appears as disengagement may instead reflect a mismatch between the institutional worlds people inhabit and the conceptual templates researchers use to interpret them.

1. The problem of recognition

At the heart of the issue is a problem of recognition. Many inherited models of civic engagement assume a relatively coherent public arena. They imagine a world in which the state is legible, institutional channels are reasonably stable, and participation can be observed through recognised acts directed at clearly defined authorities.

But everyday urban life often unfolds differently. Authority may be fragmented. Access to officials may be inconsistent. Formal channels may be slow, costly, absent, or strategically irrelevant. Residents may rely on brokers, religious figures, neighbours, informal leaders, family networks, or practical negotiation rather than moving through official pathways. They may engage intensely with collective life without appearing in the categories most often used to measure engagement.

When this happens, the analytical mistake is subtle but serious. Researchers do not merely miss activity. They risk misclassifying adaptation as absence.

2. Why the category of disengagement is too quick

The language of disengagement often appears neutral. In practice, it can carry a quiet judgement. It suggests withdrawal, passivity, indifference, or a weak civic ethic. Sometimes those interpretations are warranted. But often they are not.

In uncertain and uneven urban settings, people may withhold from formal public channels not because they do not care, but because they have learned, through experience, that those channels do not reliably produce response, protection, or repair. They may appear sceptical, distant, or selective because engagement, as conventionally defined, is expensive and uncertain.

In such contexts, practical reason matters. Residents ask: which authority can actually act? Which route is worth the effort? Which relationship is likely to deliver? What is visible in a survey instrument as “low participation” may be, from the resident’s point of view, a rational adjustment to institutional conditions.

3. A structure-first reading

This paper proposes a different starting point. Instead of asking first whether people are participating enough, we should ask what kind of public world they are inhabiting. What institutions are present? Which are absent? Where are access points blocked, costly, or unreliable? Which actors hold practical authority? Under what conditions does formal engagement become worthwhile, and when does it become secondary to more relational or improvised forms of navigation?

This shift matters because it moves explanation away from presumed civic deficiency and toward institutional configuration. It treats people less as isolated bearers of attitudes and more as practical interpreters of the environments they must move through.

A structure-first approach does not romanticise informality. Nor does it deny the value of formal democratic participation. Rather, it asks how actual civic repertoires are shaped by the design, accessibility, and credibility of the institutional order.

4. Civic life beyond the usual indicators

Once we loosen the grip of narrow recognition, a different picture comes into view. Civic life may include problem-solving through social networks, repeated negotiation with local brokers, tactical engagement with multiple authorities, low-visibility mutual support, selective compliance, strategic silence, guarded trust, or pragmatic forms of cooperation oriented toward survival and everyday stability.

None of these should be treated as inherently emancipatory. Some may reproduce hierarchy, exclusion, or dependence. But that is precisely why they must be analysed rather than dismissed. If we only recognise civic life when it appears in normatively preferred forms, we blind ourselves to how public life is actually organised.

5. Implications for urban research

The implications are methodological as much as theoretical. Survey-based research on participation often depends on indicators that travel poorly across different institutional settings. Questions may be technically clear while remaining conceptually narrow. Researchers then compare places using measures that assume a shared civic grammar where none may exist.

This does not mean measurement should be abandoned. It means measurement must become more reflexive. We need stronger conceptual discipline around what our indicators actually capture and what they leave out. We also need more humility when moving from observed behaviour to claims about civic commitment.

Put plainly: low expression in recognised channels should not automatically be read as low public orientation.

6. Toward a broader civic vocabulary

A broader civic vocabulary would not discard formal participation. It would place it within a wider repertoire of public action. It would recognise that people build and defend collective life under conditions that are often institutionally fractured, politically uneven, and socially demanding.

It would also force a more careful question onto urban scholarship: not simply whether people participate, but how public life becomes possible under conditions of uncertainty.

That question is especially important in African cities, where mobility, informality, uneven state presence, and plural authority often shape the practical terms of engagement. In these contexts, civic life may be dispersed, improvised, and relational without being empty.

7. Conclusion

The argument of this paper is modest but important. We should be more careful before calling something disengagement. Categories that appear descriptive can quietly misrecognise the worlds they claim to explain.

In many urban contexts, the issue is not the absence of civic life. It is the inadequacy of the frameworks used to see it.

A better approach begins with institutions, authority, access, and lived conditions. From there, we can ask not whether people conform to an idealised civic script, but how they actually negotiate public life in the worlds they inhabit.

This is not disengagement. It is a different civic terrain, and it requires a different analytical lens.
